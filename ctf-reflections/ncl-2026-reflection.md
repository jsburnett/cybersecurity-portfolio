# NCL 2026 – Competition Reflection

## Overview
The National Cyber League (NCL) is a biannual cybersecurity competition that uses
a Capture the Flag (CTF) format across multiple challenge domains including password
cracking, forensics, web exploitation, network analysis, cryptography, and reverse
engineering. In Spring 2026 I competed in both the Individual Game and the Team Game.

---

## Individual Game
The individual game was a chance to benchmark myself without a net. Working solo
forced me to confront gaps I might otherwise have talked through with a teammate —
particularly in areas where my instinct was right but my tooling wasn't there yet.
Juggling time constraints meant I wasn't able to put in the long hour runs like I
have in the past, which hurt me in enumeration, web, and forensics — which proved
to be the most challenging categories for me. It was a humbling and motivating
experience in equal measure.

## Team Game

### The Team
Unbelievably happy with my teams performance this year. I was blessed to act as team
captain and I had the privilege of working with six outstanding teammates from all 
over the globe. Every time I compete with a team from SANS I am more and more impressed
by the skill and expertise that students of this organization bring to the table. I am
always humbled by my team in the best way possible — this team was no exception. Truly
an outstanding group of highly skilled individuals that any organization would be lucky
to have.

### How We Organized
Rather than diving in blind, we established a lightweight workflow to track progress
and avoid duplicating effort:

- Used a shared Google spreadsheet broken out by challenge category to log status,
  coordinate work, and track correct and incorrect answers
- Divided initial attempts by domain based on individual strengths
- Communicated primarily within a private Discord server, using dedicated threads
  for each challenge category

### What Worked
- Clear ownership per challenge prevented overlap and kept momentum going
- Sharing partial progress openly meant someone else could often pick up where
  another left off
- The spreadsheet was the real MVP for communication and coordination — it not only
  showed who was working on what, it helped each person quickly identify where to
  focus their energy

### What I'd Do Differently
- The only thing I'd change next time is scheduling more group calls toward the end
  to tackle the tougher challenges together. I think working through some of those
  problems in real time as a group would have made a real difference.

### Takeaways
Competing as part of a team adds a layer of complexity that the individual game
doesn't have — coordination, communication, and trust matter just as much as
technical skill. Piggybacking off the section above, I am notoriously anti-social,
which is a real issue in situations like this. In hindsight, I always feel like I'd
be more successful if I had more confidence to get on a call and work through things
with people. I hide behind a keyboard way too much.
